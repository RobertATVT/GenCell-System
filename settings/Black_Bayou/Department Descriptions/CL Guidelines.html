Guidelines for interacting with members under 18 years of age ("Minors"):

Community Leaders must comply with the following guidelines when interacting with minors online.

• Personal Identifying Information (PII)

At no time should a Community Leader solicit or collect PII from a minor. In addition, Community Leaders should take measures to prevent minors from revealing PII about themselves. This may include removing posts on message boards or reminding kids in a chat room not to share any PII.

PII includes any of the follow:

- Full Name
- Home Telephone Number
- Home Address (any part of, including zip code)
- School Name
- Parents Name(s)
- Social Security Number
- Child's Photo and any of the above
- Child's Photo, Screen Name/E-mail


• Communicating with minors: Do's and Don'ts

Online
- Don't initiate or engage in individual one-on-one communications with a minor. This includes but is not limited to Instant Messages or email.
Note: An exception applies to those who are involved with Ask a Teacher or Homework Help.

- If a minor is attempting to initiate individual contact with you Do encourage them to interact with their peers online instead. Suggest the use of chat or message boards.

- Don't market any services or products to a minor.

- Do direct children to use KW: Tell AOL when they are in need of help.

Offline
- Don't ever initiate or carrying out any type of offline contact with a minor. This includes but is not limited to phone calls or face-to-face meetings.

- If a minor asks for an offline meeting or desires help Do encourage them to talk to a parent or teacher.

Legal Threats:

If a member threatening legal action addresses you, forward the mail/IM log to your online coordinator. Do not correspond with this member. The AOL Legal Department or Partner will review the situation.

Newsletter Tips

It has come to our attention that some volunteers who distribute solicited newsletters are being reported to TOS because a member has mistaken the publication for SPAM.

To prevent this from occurring from this point forward, we have put together a "tips list" for you to reference when creating your newsletters. These tips have all been included in this list because they address some of the top reasons why newsletters are mistaken for SPAM. If you are responsible for distributing a solicited newsletter to the members in your forum, feel free to review these tips and incorporate them into your future publications.


Top Eleven Tips



The following tips are recommended for any Community Leader who is sending solicited e-mail to AOL members. These tips are designed to encourage high subscription and member satisfaction levels, and to help ensure that your e-mail is not mistaken for Spam by the member.

Tip #1: Mass mailings should be free of charge to the member, observe TOS and AOL's Privacy Policy. All mailings to AOL members must be solicited.

Tip #2: Differentiate your mailing from spam through consistency, frequency, and personality.
Send your mailing on the same day. Send your mailing at least once every two weeks. Use an e-mail address that reflects the content/personality of your area and use it consistently. Alert members before you change delivery times, frequency, or sending screen name.

Consistency and alerts help protect you against impersonation. Personality and frequency make the mailing more relevant to the member.

Tip #3: Keep it short.
Keep your mailing to one printed page or less. Length is the number one reason why a member deletes or unsubscribes from a newsletter.

Tip #4: Do not include attached files or embedded images in your mailings.

Tip #5: Include obvious, easy to use unsubscribe directions in your mailing. A one click unsubscribe process is ideal.

Example: That's it for this week! I'll be back next week with more tips, links, and special deals! To unsubscribe from this newsletter go to Keyword: C&S News and click "Unsubscribe."

Tip #6: Your mailing should be solicited, but it is important to regularly remind members that the mailing that they are receiving is free and that they did subscribe to it.
Many members forget that they've subscribed to a mailing. Regularly reinforce the concept that the member subscribed to the mailing and that it is private -- the member's e-mail address will not be sold or distributed to an outside party.

Example: PS: You are receiving AOL's International Channel Newsletter, "Around the World with AOL" because you subscribed to it. Your subscription to our newsletter is free and confidential (we don't like junk e-mail either)! You may unsubscribe from the newsletter at any time. Simply click here and then click the Unsubscribe button.

Tip #7: Use your mailing as an incentive for members to visit your area (not as an alternate content delivery vehicle).
If a member receives all of the content of your area in their mailbox, there is no reason for him/her to visit your area. Limiting your mailing to one printed page and using hyperlinks in context will help you achieve this goal. In order to ensure that members remain focused on your core message, try not to include more than 5 hyperlinks per e-mail.

Tip #8: Provide instructions on using hyperlinks and keywords -- not all members know how to use them. Also, be sure to hyperlink to areas on AOL (not the web).
Example: Newsletter tip: Click on the blue underlined words to go directly to our featured travel areas.

Tip #9: Remember that members don't read mail as soon as it is sent.
Be sure that your hyperlinks last for at least one week. If your mailing contains a time sensitive offer, clearly communicate the expiration date.

Tip #10: Make your subscription form simple and easy to use.
Describe your newsletter to members and make it clear that it's free and delivered by
e-mail. Provide members with a one-click subscription process.

Example: The Weekly Byte

Tip #11: Communicate with the member as soon as s/he subscribes or unsubscribes from your mailing.
Send a short confirmation/welcome message to newly subscribed members; send a "good-bye" message to members who've asked to be removed from your distribution list, encourage them to visit your area.

Problem Resolution

AOL makes every attempt to maintain a positive, productive, and fair environment. Part of this commitment is encouraging an open and honest atmosphere in which problems, complaints, suggestions, or questions receive a timely response from ACI supervisors and management. Community Leaders are encouraged to offer positive and constructive criticism.

In any organization, misunderstandings or conflicts can arise. It is important to address concerns BEFORE serious problems develop. If you find yourself in a situation that you feel needs extra attention, the following problem resolution procedure has been developed for your use as a Community Leader:

(Community Leaders will not be penalized, formally or informally, for voicing a complaint in regards to policy, or practice with AOL in a reasonable, and courteous manner, or for using the problem resolution procedure.) *The Community Leader may discontinue the procedure at any step.

1. If possible, first discuss the issue with the other person(s) involved. There are two sides to almost every situation; take the time to consider both.

2. Should the issue require further attention, AOL/ACI encourages Community Leaders to seek assistance from their immediate supervisor

3. If the issue is still not resolved, talk with the leader of the Forum for final resolution.

** As always, the Community Leaders Organization and their Managers maintain an open door policy. Coming soon is a process to assist Community Leaders with resolution issues that are in need of special attention.

Professional Behavior:

The Community Leader golden rule: Treat others, as you would want them to treat you. Deal directly and honestly with your fellow volunteers while demonstrating the thought process that everyone's opinion matters.

You have the opportunity daily, to cooperate and work with others to enhance the member experience.

A few starting points:
1. Your Community Leader screen name is to be used only while you are wearing your volunteer "hat", (i.e., only when acting as a volunteer). All screen names on your Community Leader account are expected to comply with the guidelines set forth in this document.

2. Interactions with AOL members must be handled in a professional manner. Harassing, or arguing with any AOL member, employee, or Community Leader is not permitted, and will be considered grounds for immediate release as a Community Leader. (See above golden rule)

Time Away:

While Community Leaders are volunteers, keeping your commitment to your shift mates, peers, teams and AOL members is important.

CLO/ACI understands there are emergencies and other situations that may call you away from your volunteer commitments. As a result, we have created the following Guidelines for "Time Away". Time away is defined as any reason that precludes you from volunteering the scheduled hours to which you have committed.

If you are planning to take time away, please contact your online coordinator at least two weeks in advance, if possible, so you can arrange to have your volunteer commitments covered. At the end of the agreed upon time away, we assume that you will return to your volunteer commitments.

We understand that medical emergencies and other situations may arise and you might need extensive time away from your volunteer duties. Please contact your online coordinator to let them know. If after 2 months, you have not been able to resume as a volunteer, you will need to delete your Community Leader name, and your account will be converted to billable status. If you wish to return to the volunteer program, please contact your online coordinator. Your account will be reviewed to determine if there is still a need for the particular position, as well as any necessary retraining requirements.

~Harassment
Harassment is not acceptable behavior for any AOL volunteer. America Online considers harassment a very serious issue. Respecting the people you volunteer with, their individuality and opinions is vital to making this arrangement successful.

Formally defined, harassment is any action - including derogatory comments, inappropriate gestures, offensive visual material or hostile behavior - that another individual finds offensive. Discrimination on the basis of race, religion, national origin, age, sex, sexual orientation, disability or any other personal characteristic will not be tolerated within the AOL volunteer Community.

If you feel you have been harassed, you should tell the person how you feel - if you feel comfortable doing so. If not, contact your online coordinator (or the next highest manager with whom you feel comfortable discussing the incident) immediately. Your claim will be investigated thoroughly, and no repercussive actions will be made toward you.

Individuals who engage in any form of harassment face serious consequences, up to and including release from the volunteer program.
