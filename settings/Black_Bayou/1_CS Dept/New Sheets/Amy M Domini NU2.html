Background


 Childhood, she will say if asked, is of little consequence. She will say that nothing that happened then had any effect on what is happening now. She will defend anyone except herself - but blame the same people she refuses to speak of if pushed in the right ways. Halfway through the time-honored thing we call life, her father left. He simply walked away. It was all terribley straight-forward: first he goes crazy, then he leaves. That's all there was to it. Life went on as it had before: the clock in the corner ticked; the sun rose and set; she huddled in corners because corners had always been her favorite places. She adored people but found herself at the edges of rooms, always. She hated dealing with people and needed to be around them constantly.
She was born in Voletta and attended public school, but couldn't give you the names of the people in her class if life hung on those facts. She kept to herself; ignored - with all due grace - the world. She never had an outburst in school; never spoke unless spoken to; had all her work in on time - would have been a teacher's dream, except for a shoddy attendance record and a refusal to participate in very many activities. She routinely skipped lunch to play the piano.
So no one ever found out: that the scratches on her arms weren't accidental cat-claw marks. She, so carefully, never cut deep enough to leave lasting scars. It would be a shame if that happened - because then she would be crazy, and she just wasn't allowed to be crazy. She graduated from high school with a brief psychiatric record - "request discharge; no diagnosis." Therapy had been uneventful.
Terrified of displeasing anyone (because then, you see, they would abandon her), she entered the state university in September 1999. Everything was fine for a while. In early February 2000 her on-again/off-again boyfriend (she had a lot of those) left. She exhausted him. She was too picky. She wouldn't change. She nodded - this was true. It was only proving all of her obsessive theorizing right. We're all so cyclic, she'd say; we all spiral, over and over and over, back and forth - not like a pendulum; a pendulum is far too orderly. It's more like crooked tide lines.
By the twentieth, she'd taken a couple of Valium and somewhere between fifty and sixty Asprin (she lost count; it was supposed to be sixty) and had her stomach pumped - twice. Though it most certainly was not a result of the breakup, that separation gave her a most convenient excuse. She never actually thought that relationship would last, anyway. The second time she OD'd, there was no way to work around admitting her to the psych ward. In looking for an identity, she had one handed to her - the identity of a mixed up little girl; how unfortunate that she accepted this one; how unfortunate that no one has tried to convince her there's more to it than that. And this - is where we pick up.
