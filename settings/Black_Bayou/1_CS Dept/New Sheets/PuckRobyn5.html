Background


Picture growing up in the perfect life on Isle Voletta. You have friends by the score, boys chase after you, and you let them. You have loving parents, and a good summer job. You've pulled excellent grades in shool, and have nearly a perfect attendance record (heck, nobody expects you to go in with strep thorat and the measles, do they?). You're popular, and your parents think you're the perfect daughter. But somehow you don't feel like the perfect daughter. You're more of a tomboy than any of the other girls were growing up. You have something of a violent streak, though you're slow to anger. You like the boys chasing you, but you wouldn't mind the girls joining in either, which is downright scarry, given how you father and mother think of such things. You're tougher than any of the other girls. So much so that your younger siblings, a boy and a girl, get teased constantly about their "Big Brother Robin." So you, Robin Elizabeth Goodfellow, are a little put off by your own actions, but blow them off as just being something that you'll grow out of.
That was how life was for Robin until she turned 17. It was springtime, and the last play of the year, "A Midsummer Night's Dream," was going to be showing for three nights at the highschool theater. Robin was one of the stars of the show- she got the part of Puck, or Robin Goodfellow, much to the amusement of the teachers, staff, and other students. She was looking forward to the preformance, but had been severely troubled by the fact that, at 17, she had yet to develope fully. Her mother took her to the doctor for a routine well-woman checkup, Robin's first, and the results were disasterous. The doctor informed her, on the afternoon before the opening preformance, that "she" was not female.

((In utero, all fetuses, whether male or female, start out with a predisposition to be female, in spite of the chromosomes. The only thing that prevents a male from developing as a female are certain "turn on" genes. When these genes fail to work in the uterus, the infant, in spite of having a completely normal XY chromosome pattern, developes a female body. The penis is non-existant, and the testicals develope within the body, rendering them usless. Small breasts develope due to lack of testosterone development by the testicals, but the child does not cycle, having no overies or uterus. This is what happened to Robin. She lost her mind.
The next memory she had was a few minutes later, with her mother and the doctor both gripping their ears in pain, though she'd not heard a sound made from anyone. Apparently she'd "screamed" in her mind... and the doctor and her mother both heard it in theirs. About that time she figured that no matter what else, she was *not* going home. While the two adults were still concussed, she grabbed her clothes and split. She didn't go home that night, nor any night after that. Instead she ran away and lived with one of her older friends at her apartment. She dropped out of school and started working in a laundromat for minimum wage on a parttime basis. While she had free time she studied at the library, both about her condition and about her new-found ability to "hear" other people talking in her head. She slowly lost her grip on reality, and as her 18th birthday passed, she was no longer completely mentally stable. Her identity of herself split into the "male" Puck and the "female" Robin, though she doesn't have multiple personalities. S/he simply thinks of him/herself as being male, female, or both at any given moment. S/he has a limited understanding of what a psychic is and what her powers are. What understanding she does have is a bit skewed by her perception that she is actually Puck Robin Goodfellow, the fairy from "A Midsummer Night's Dream." S/he believes in magic and in otherworldly creatures, but without any proof save what her twisted mind was able to put together from her reading. In spite of her insanity, she's a friendly, cheerful person, very courteous and kind, and rather loving.
