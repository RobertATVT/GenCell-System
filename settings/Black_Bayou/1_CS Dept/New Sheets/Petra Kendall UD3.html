Physical Description


 Petra Kendall is 4'6" and weighs 70 pounds. She is slim, bird-boned, frail of build, and weak of constitution. Her eyes are a pale peridot green, with amber flecks, and her knee-length hair is the color of the dawn, a rosy reddish-gold. The few outfits she owns from her father are cast-off, second-hand jeans and t-shirts, sweaters in winter--stained, badly frayed, far out of style. Thin, fading scars mar her silken porcelain skin, and over a period of eighteen years, she has endured having almost every major bone in her body broken, many of them more than once. Despite that, she has healed well, and when cleaned up, even in cast-off clothes, she is beautiful enough--in a tiny, birdlike way--to take a person's breath away. Slightly tilted eyes frame a straight, slender nose; full rosebud lips are turned down with sorrow or fear far more often than uptilted in a joyous smile. High cheekbones and a narrow, foxlike chin complete the delicate features, and her limbs and hands are thin and waiflike. Her figure is svelte, a far cry from the voluptuous forms of those such as Anna Nicole Smith or Pamela Lee Anderson, but she is undeniably feminine, a waifish child-woman of the sort that misogynistic sociopaths consider the perfect victim.

Background

Petra Kendall is a tiny china doll of a girl, abused throughout her entire life by the drunken wretch of a father who blamed her for the fact that her mother died during childbirth. Her growth stunted and her frail body habitually weak from poor nutrition and lack of food as she was growing up, she survived such a life only because of a combination of luck and the fact that, just when such beatings seemed enough to kill her, something or someone would intervene. Despite the visible bruises on her face and hands, the casts on broken arms or legs, Petra slipped through the cracks of an overburdened Social Services Agency which should have taken her away from her father many years ago.
Petra's one chance for escape came during one of her father's many bouts of drunkenness. After she had done her chores for the evening, he customarily locked her in the closet where she had spent the first eighteen years of her life. That closet, with its pallet on the floor and single sheet, with no light and no pillow, was her only home, and she had spent her entire life there. School was only a dream to her; she had never been in a car, or to church, or to a park; she had not even been toilet-trained until she was eight years old, and she could not write or read, not even her own name. She returned to her closet, curling up to go to sleep, and her father came storming into the kitchen with a glass he had been drinking beer out of which she had not washed. Forbidden by her father since she was old enough to understand speech that she was forbidden to go into the living room--where he sat drinking and watching TV, which she had never seen--she had been unable to collect the glass. For this Catch-22, he began to beat her. Before he could get very far, however, he passed out. Bruised and bleeding, Petra gathered her few meager belongings and fled, trying to put as much distance between herself and her father, as he would certainly seek her out once he woke.
Petra has been on the road since then, not quite a runaway, but homeless, moving from place to place, certain her father is still looking for her--as he is. Having never had any education at all, there are very few businesses, even fast-food places, that will hire her, and so she works mostly in orchards and fields with migrant laborers, making barely enough to keep from starving, fending off the advances of strangers, and always moving on before too much time has passed, to make sure her father isn't too close behind. In the last two years, she has taught herself to read and write, and learned a love of music that has helped to keep most of her sanity intact.
And now her wandering travels have brought her to Voletta.
