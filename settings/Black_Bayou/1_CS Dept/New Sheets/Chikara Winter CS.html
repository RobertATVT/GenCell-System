Physical Description

Fine, long hair once the color of the midnight sky is entirely silver now, and her hands are knotted and arthritic. She wears old woolen skirts and shawls in Native American patterns over faded cotton blouses--white or grey--and moccasins. A myriad of silver and turquoise jewelry adorns her bird-boned wrists & her wrinkled throat and ears. 4'2", 61 lbs.

Background


Born April 1, 1898, Chikara Winterhawk has followed the path of Raven and Coyote--the trickster, who marked her as his own when she was born on the day of the Fool, or April Fool's Day, as the White Man calls it--all her life. Chikara's father was the shaman for his tribe from the age of 16 until he died at age 70. He had no son to follow in his footsteps, only a daughter--Chikara, who was 23 when he died. Chikara was first in so many things--first member of the tribe to go to high school, much less first woman to do so--that they didn't bother to protest when she took over as the shaman for the tribe. Things at the time were changing for women, including the right to vote, and she had learned at her father's knee all he knew--the tribe's legends, mythology, history, culture, healing ways, rites of blessing, rituals and medicine man training. She is 102 now...and has come to Isle Voletta in search of her grand-daughter and only remaining relative, Rya Winter.
Chikara is patient and pragmatic, willing to go to insane lengths to learn new things--as evidenced by her attending a white school in the first place, an unheard-of thing for an Indian woman in the 1910's. Her upbringing was sufficiently harsh that she does indeed carry grudges and/or a prejudice against whites in general, but she is also open-minded enough not to let it interfere with her getting new information when she seeks it, or any goal she might pursue. She is honest to a fault, blunt (but can be tactful when the occasion calls for it), quiet around strangers, curious about everything going on in the world around her, and intensely devoted to the few friends she has. She is slow to trust, especially outsiders, but once trust is given, it lasts forever unless one does something to shatter it. Finally, knowing all her tribe's legends about Indian demons and monsters, she is curious about finding out about corresponding myths the whites have...and so studies the white tales of the 'devil' and his ilk--vampires, werewolves, etc...whenever a chance arises.
Chikara can speak to the spirits, and they speak back to her, having granted her many of the gifts of the ancient wonder-workers of the Native American people--the ability to change her shape, the ability to see in the dark like an animal, the ability to heal her wounds as if they were smoke, and speak in the minds of others. At the age of 102, Chikara Winterhawk is -ancient-...but that makes her no less formidable an opponent, for she has had decades in which to gather the information to use against any foes that might harm her or the ones she loves.

> The coyote is somewhat smaller than the wolf; most individuals measure
> 28 to 38 inches (70 to 97 centimeters) in head-body length, and stand 18 to
> 21 inches (45 to 538 centimeters) tall at shoulder height; average weight is
> between 25 and 33 pounds (12 and 15 kilograms). Males tend to be larger than
> females. The coat is usually a grizzled grayish brown, often with a patch of
> white at the throat and belly. The muzzle is narrow and generally slightly
> darker in color. The tail tip often has a black patch.
>
> Like the wolf, the coyote's basic social unit is the breeding pair. The
> image of the coyote as a solitary animal is somewhat erroneous. Although the
> animals often hunt alone, they may form packs in some situations,
> particularly when a major clumped food resource is readily available. In
> these circumstances, when the relative abundance of food can support larger
> groups of the animals, packs are formed by the delayed dispersal of offspring.
> Rather than leaving their family when they are old enough to mate, young
> adult coyotes will remain with the pack, where their primary responsibility
> is to protect subsequent offspring from predation. Often the young adults do
> not leave to start their own families until they are 2or 3 years old.
>
> Female coyotes produce an annual litter of usually six pups, which are
> born blind and helpless and are nursed for up to seven weeks. In situations
> where formation of a pack is not advantageous, the young will disperse during
> their first year, traveling as far as 100 miles (160 kilometers) from home to
> find a mate and settle down.
>
> The diet of the coyote is primarily mammalian, particularly jack rabbits
> and rodents. However, coyote also consume large ungulates such as deer and
> antelope, as well as insects and fruit. Primarily active at dusk and
> nighttime, coyote generally stalk prey from only a few meters away. However,
> they are capable of chasing their prey some distance, and can achieve speeds
> as fast as 40 miles (64 kilometers) per hour.
>
> Their adaptability in social organization as well as diet has enabled the
> coyote to thrive in a large variety of habitats. Coyotes have historically
> been found over much of western North America; however, by the 1990s
> increased development of formerly pristine, or near-pristine, areas along
> with increased human habitation of the latter resulted in a decreased number
> of larger predators such as bears and the big cats. As the risk of predation
> decreased, coyote populations increased in size, requiring an expansion to
> new territories. In particular, coyotes began to migrate eastward. Near the
> close of the 20th century, spotting a coyote in a suburban, or even sometimes
> highly urbanized, area was not an uncommon event.
