Background

An overzealous curl sprang back from the restraint of the shell-shaped ear it had just been tucked behind as Chris noted the rubenesque form taking up residence beside her desk. Frigid blue eyes, that reveal a warm violet hue on closer inspection, shift across liquid chocolate pools teaming with unshed tears and french manicure boasting hands wringing relentlessly at one another.

"Jeanne, you know I have to do this, hon."

"I know." Even when strained due to a fight for control, Jeanne still had a voice Chris would give anything for; a deep melodious voice that sometimes seemed more a sigh formed into words. "The kids won't understand, though. No one is as good with them as you are."

"Not true, you work just as hard with them as I do. You should stop underestimating your work." In a characteristic attempt at calming the woman beside her down, Chris rose to her feet and closed her arms about the shorter woman, sympathetic tears springing unbidden into her own eyes. Jeanne had been a major part of the last 7 years of her life and she was no more prepared to leave the woman behind than her friend was to accept it. Her first day on the ranch had brought her into contact with the feisty, outspoken woman who could be brutally honest and put you at ease in the same moment. The glossy sheen of her newly acquired psychology diploma was no protection against Jeanne's praise and criticism, both given in the same comforting tones.

"It won't be the same."

The Ranch, a place where badly behaved and unwanted boys were sent as a last resort,
boasted a wide range of counselors whose skills papered the walls in the form of diplomas and pictures from the boys who had managed to graduate college or find their nitche in other ways. Jeanne had ridden her during the first 3 months of her term and had spent the remaining years using her as an example for newer employees.

For years, as a child in Montana, she had concentrated solely on her skills as a barrel racer. Robert and Christina Stanford watched as she progressed from her first pony, to a gelding, to a spirited, headstrong chestnut mare. Horses were like an extension of herself and riding them came almost as second nature. By 12 she had won more competitions than her mother and had charmed the judges into allowing her to race in a higher age bracket. A choice they did not regret.

In November of her 18th year a half-buried log ruined her dreams. The impact of a 14 hand horse falling upon her leg should have crippled her for life. However, the cushion of 3 feet of snow made certain that, while she would not be winning any further competitions, she would walk.

Psychology became her comfort. School managed to blunt her restlessness and feed her mind as she learned again how to ride and manage the horses she so loved. Frequently angry over her loss. Upon graduation, Christine was employed at, what was fondly called The Ranch, and spent the next seven years of her life there. Due to the frequency of her work bringing her into contact with boys considered deliquents, she became involved in many legal aspects as well. Soon she found herself rallying beside them in court and dealing one on one with their probation, and in rare cases, their parole officers. This lead to an interest in legal psychology and drove her to move to Isle Voletta Louisiana where she intends to apply for the position of staff psychologist at the local police department.
