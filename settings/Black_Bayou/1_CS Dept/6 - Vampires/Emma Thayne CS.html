Emma Thayne was born on a January evening 21 years ago. She was born deaf. Her parents, Magdelena and Gregory Thayne didn't learn of her affliction until she was two years old. They considered themselves blessed with a child who never cried.. yet, oddly enough, never cooed or laughed. When the doctor proclaimed her deaf, it sent them into shock. They tried every way possibly to prove that she could hear their voices, the sounds they made.. but nothing worked. Instead of having to live with a child so detached from all they knew, they sent her to a live-in school for the deaf/mute. She grew up in a place not her home, she skipped down halls painted a stale gray instead of the violet she dreamed of, and she went on smiling despite the silence inherent to her gaze. She learned to draw pictures in the air as a means of communication, sign language, as they called it. She learned like any other student, through reading and writing and understanding. She learned to read lips quickly, and when she got older, received a hearing aide. The hearing aide enabled her to learn the simplest vowels an consonants, providing her with a semblence of broken speech. But she hated this tool, she hated the look in people's eyes as they attempted to understand her words. And found that drawing her air pictures was much easier. She became an interpreter for deaf children when she turned 18, and she'd only seen her parents a total of ten times her entire life. She assumed that all families were this way. Afterall, the stories were all the same in the school where she lived.
After her 18th birthday, she moved out of the school and found an apartment. She had learned to play the piano at The School, but it had never occured to her that she could play anywhere else. The apartment she'd rented came furnished, complete with an old, worn piano who's chords were slightly out of tune. But it was a piano. Her fingers descended upon the ivory of its keys, following the vibrations delt by the strumming chords as the checkerboard of its notes fell beneath her imagination. She could not hear the beauty of its song, but she could feel it; she could imagine it, and make it a reality.
She moved through life in this way. She maintained work at the school as an interpreter, and played concerts in the evenings for a crowd who left shivering from the lingering sounds of her music. An each time they approached to relay a compliment, the pianist's hands would lift, crooking and twisting in a dance of humble acceptance. "Don't thank me, just enjoy my gift", she would sign. And someone who had been to more than one concert would arrive by her side, and translate as best they could to the newcomer. That is how she taught the ignorant. Through generosity, and patience. She knew that every sign made sense when people bothered to watch. So, please, watch.


A small school for disabled children recently opened in a small town off the coast of Louisiana. They need helpers badly, and the now 21 Emma has decided to stop in and offer her services to those who need it.
